Setup Guide

This guide explains the three trade setup patterns that FuturesEdge AI detects in real time — what they mean, what they look like on a chart, and how to act on them. No experience required.

What FuturesEdge AI does

Scans MNQ and MGC futures across five timeframes (1m–15m), detects high-probability setups, and scores their quality from 0–100%.

What it does NOT do

It never executes trades. It surfaces information to help you make better decisions — the final call is always yours.

Three setup types

Liquidity Sweep Reversal, Zone Rejection, and Break of Structure / Change of Character. All three are explained below.

How to use this guide: Read the candlestick basics first if you're new to charts. Then study each setup. When an alert fires on the dashboard, come back here to remind yourself what the pattern means and what confirms it.

Reading Candlesticks

Every bar on the chart is a candlestick — a compact summary of price action over one time period (1 minute, 5 minutes, etc.).

A green (bullish) candle closed higher than it opened. A red (bearish) candle closed lower. The thin lines are "wicks" showing the highest and lowest price reached during that period.

Body vs. Wick

The body (the thick rectangle) shows where price opened and closed. The wick (the thin line) shows the extremes reached during the candle — but price didn't stay there.

A long wick with a small body is called a hammer or shooting star — it means price was rejected at that level.

Timeframes

Each candle represents one time period. On the 1-minute chart, each candle = 1 minute. On the 15-minute chart, each candle = 15 minutes.

Higher timeframes (15m) show the big picture. Lower timeframes (1m, 5m) show the entry trigger. FuturesEdge AI monitors all five simultaneously.

Key Concepts

Before diving into the setups, here are the building blocks you'll see referenced in every alert.

Swing Highs and Swing Lows

A swing high is a peak — a candle whose high is higher than the candles on either side. A swing low is a valley. These are the natural turning points in price and act as reference levels for every setup.

Order Blocks (OB)

An order block is the last opposing candle before a strong, impulsive price move. For example, the last bearish candle before a big rally upward. Institutional traders often leave large pending orders at these levels — when price returns to them, it frequently reacts strongly. The system marks them as untested, tested, or mitigated.

Fair Value Gaps (FVG)

A fair value gap is a three-candle sequence where price moves so fast that a visible gap is left between candle 1's high and candle 3's low (bullish FVG) or between candle 1's low and candle 3's high (bearish FVG). Markets tend to return to fill these gaps before continuing. An open FVG means price hasn't returned yet.

Market Regime

The system classifies each timeframe as either trend (price is making consistent higher highs/lows or lower highs/lows) or range (price is bouncing between support and resistance). Setups that align with the regime score higher confidence.

Multi-timeframe alignment: The best setups occur when the 15m and 5m timeframes agree on direction. The system flags this as an alignment bonus in the confidence score.

Setup 1

Liquidity Sweep Reversal

Plain language: Price briefly spikes past a known swing high or swing low — grabbing the stop-loss orders sitting just beyond it — then immediately reverses direction and closes back inside the range. This spike is the "sweep."

Retail traders place their stops just above swing highs and just below swing lows. Institutional players know this, and they briefly push price to those levels to trigger a flood of orders, fill their own positions at better prices, and then let price move the other direction.

Bearish sweep: price spikes above the swing high (amber candle), triggers stops, then reverses sharply. Entry on the next bearish candle.

What to look for (Bearish)

  • Clear swing high visible on chart
  • Price wicks above that high (even briefly)
  • Closes back below the swing high in same candle
  • Next candle confirms with bearish body
  • 15m or 5m regime is bearish or neutral

What to look for (Bullish)

  • Clear swing low visible on chart
  • Price wicks below that low (stop run)
  • Closes back above the swing low
  • Next candle confirms with bullish body
  • Demand zone or FVG nearby adds confidence

What invalidates this setup

  • Price does not close back inside the range after the sweep
  • Multiple candles close beyond the swing level (breakout, not sweep)
  • The stop-loss level is breached before entry is triggered

Key insight: The sweep candle itself is NOT the entry. Wait for the confirmation candle that closes in the direction of the reversal. Chasing the sweep candle is a common mistake.

Setup 2

Supply / Demand Zone Rejection

Plain language: Price enters a previously identified area where large institutional orders were placed (an Order Block or Fair Value Gap), then shows a clear rejection — a candle with a long wick pointing into the zone and a small body closing back outside it.

These zones represent levels where big buyers (demand) or big sellers (supply) previously stepped in. When price returns to the same level, those same institutions often re-enter, causing a sharp reaction.

Bullish zone rejection: price drops into a demand zone, produces a hammer candle (long lower wick), then rallies. Entry at the close of the confirmation candle.

The rejection candle — what it looks like

Bullish rejection (demand zone)

Long lower wick pointing down into the zone. Small body at the top. Closes above the zone's upper boundary. Called a hammer.

Bearish rejection (supply zone)

Long upper wick pointing up into the zone. Small body at the bottom. Closes below the zone's lower boundary. Called a shooting star.

What to look for

  • Order Block or FVG zone clearly identified on the chart
  • Price enters the zone (wick or body)
  • Rejection candle has wick ≥ 2× body length into the zone
  • Body closes back outside the zone
  • Next candle moves away from the zone (confirmation)
  • Higher-timeframe regime agrees with direction

Zone quality matters: Order blocks that caused a strong, fast impulsive move away from them are higher quality than weak reactions. The system scores OB quality automatically and includes it in the confidence score.

Setup 3

Break of Structure & Change of Character

Plain language: In a trending market, price makes a series of higher highs and higher lows (uptrend) or lower highs and lower lows (downtrend). A Break of Structure (BOS) is when price closes beyond the last significant swing point in the direction of the trend — confirming continuation. A Change of Character (CHoCH) is when price closes beyond a swing point in the opposite direction — signaling a potential trend reversal.

BOS (bullish): price closes above the prior swing high, confirming the uptrend continues. Entry at the close of the breakout candle or the next candle open.

CHoCH (bearish): price fails to make a new high (lower high), then closes below the most recent swing low. This breaks the uptrend structure — a reversal is starting.

BOS vs. CHoCH — quick comparison

BOS — Break of Structure

Trend continuation. Price closes beyond a swing point in the same direction as the current trend. Use it to enter or add to a position in the direction of the trend.

CHoCH — Change of Character

Reversal warning. Price closes beyond a swing point against the current trend. Use it to anticipate a trend flip. Higher risk — wait for confirmation before entering.

CHoCH confirmation: A CHoCH alert alone is not enough to enter. Wait for price to pull back and show a zone rejection or sweep setup in the new direction. Jumping in immediately on a CHoCH is high risk.

Risk Management Rules

Good setups still lose. Risk management is what keeps you in the game.

Always use a stop-loss

The system provides an invalidation level for every setup. Place your stop there. Never move a stop against you.

Risk per trade

The dollar risk display shows your exposure based on your contract count and stop distance. If it shows red, the setup is outside your max-risk budget.

R:R Ratio

Default is 2:1 (risk $1 to make $2). Higher R:R filters out more setups but only shows higher quality. Adjustable in the filter panel.

One trade at a time

The system enforces one open trade per instrument. Don't override this with multiple simultaneous positions in the same direction.

Never risk more than 1–2% of your account on a single trade. Use the contract count and max risk settings to enforce this. If a setup would require a stop that puts you over budget, skip it.

Setup quality guidelines

  • ≥ 75% — High confidence. Check regime alignment, take if setup is clean.
  • 60–74% — Moderate. Verify higher-timeframe context before entering.
  • Below 60% — Low confidence. Paper-trade or skip. Use as learning material.
