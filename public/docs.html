Setup Guide

This guide explains the three trade setup patterns that FuturesEdge AI detects in real time — what they mean, how the confidence score is calculated, and how to act on them given that market data arrives with a 15-minute delay.

What FuturesEdge AI does

Scans MNQ, MGC, MES, and MCL futures on the 5m, 15m, and 30m timeframes, detects high-probability setups, and scores their quality 0–100%.

What it does NOT do

It never executes trades. It surfaces high-quality setups to help you make better decisions — the final call is always yours.

Four alert types

Zone Rejection, PDH/PDL Breakout, Trendline Break, and Opening Range Breakout. All are explained below with diagrams and entry criteria.

15-minute data lag

Seed-mode data is at least 15 minutes old. The alert types and timeframes are specifically chosen to remain actionable despite this delay.

How to use this guide: Read the candlestick and key-concepts sections first, then the 15-minute delay section. That context makes the setup explanations much more useful. When a live alert fires, click "✦ AI" on the card for a plain-language assessment of that specific setup.

Reading Candlesticks

Every bar on the chart is a candlestick — a compact summary of price action over one time period (5 minutes, 15 minutes, 30 minutes).

A green (bullish) candle closed higher than it opened. A red (bearish) candle closed lower. The thin lines are "wicks" — the highest and lowest price touched, but not held.

Body vs. Wick

The body shows where price opened and closed. The wick shows the extreme reached during the candle — price got there but was immediately rejected. A long wick with a small body means strong rejection at that level.

Timeframes — Why We Use 5m / 15m / 30m

Higher timeframes show bigger, slower moves with more institutional weight. 5m is the fastest we scan — each candle spans 5 minutes. 30m candles each represent half an hour of market activity. With 15-min delayed data, these slower timeframes keep setups actionable.

Key Concepts

These terms appear in every alert rationale and the confidence score breakdown.

Swing Highs and Swing Lows

A swing high is a price peak where the candle's high is higher than the candles on both sides — a local maximum. A swing low is the opposite. These are natural turning points and serve as the reference for trendlines, zone boundaries, and structural breaks.

Trendlines

The system automatically draws two trendlines per timeframe:

  • Support line — anchored at the lowest low in the window, sloping upward. Connects rising swing lows.
  • Resistance line — anchored at the highest high, sloping downward. Connects falling swing highs.

The more times price has touched a trendline and bounced (more "touches"), the stronger and more meaningful a break of that line becomes. The system requires ≥3 touches before treating a crossover as a Trendline Break alert.

Order Blocks (OB)

An order block is the last opposing candle before a strong impulsive price move. For example: the last bearish candle before a big rally upward. Large institutions leave pending orders at these levels. When price returns, they often defend them — creating zone rejections.

Fair Value Gaps (FVG)

A fair value gap appears in a 3-candle sequence where price moves so fast that a visible gap remains between candle 1's extreme and candle 3's extreme. Markets tend to return to fill these gaps. When a zone rejection occurs at an open FVG, the confidence score receives an IOF (Institutional Order Flow) bonus.

Market Regime

Each timeframe is classified as trend (consistent directional movement) or range (bouncing between levels), and the direction labeled bullish, bearish, or neutral. Setups that trade with the regime earn +15 confidence points. Setups that trade against a confirmed trend are suppressed entirely — except when a CHoCH has confirmed the trend is changing.

Multi-Timeframe Alignment

When the 5m and 15m regime agree on direction, the system flags alignment as true. This earns an additional +10 confidence bonus. The best setups almost always have both timeframes pointing the same way.

Regime gate explained: If the market is in a bullish trend and alignment is true (5m + 15m both bullish), the system will only show bullish zone rejections. Bearish setups against a confirmed bullish regime are suppressed — they historically lose at a high rate and represent counter-trend noise.

Exception: if a CHoCH (Change of Character) has fired in the bearish direction, it means the structure has actually shifted. In that case, a counter-trend setup passes the gate.

What to check before entering a delayed alert

  • Look at current price on the chart — has it moved significantly past the entry level?
  • If price has run far from entry, the original R:R no longer applies — skip or wait for a re-entry candle
  • If price is still near the entry level, the setup may still be valid
  • For PDH breakouts: check whether the trend has continued or reversed; breakouts that ran 30 min ago often still have momentum
  • For trendline breaks: check if price is still above (bullish) or below (bearish) the broken trendline — if it's back inside, the break failed

Why these alert types work despite the delay

Zone Rejection (5m / 15m / 30m)

After a strong zone rejection, price typically moves away from the zone for multiple candles before reaching the TP. A 15-min delay means you may still be in the first third of the move.

PDH/PDL Breakout

Institutional acceptance of a new price range plays out over hours, not minutes. Entering a PDH breakout 15 minutes late is often still well inside the potential move — especially on MNQ with its 2:1 R:R target.

Trendline Break

Trendline breaks on 5m/15m/30m typically launch multi-candle trend moves. Entering 15 minutes after a confirmed trendline break is similar to entering on the second candle after a breakout — often the best entry anyway.

1m, 2m, and 3m charts are for visualization only. The alert feed does not scan those timeframes. By the time a 1-minute setup appears, it has already resolved (won or lost) 15 times over. The chart still displays those timeframes so you can see fine-grained price action — but no alerts are generated from them.

Alert Type 1

Supply / Demand Zone Rejection

Plain language: Price enters a previously identified area where large institutional orders sit (an Order Block or Fair Value Gap), shows a clear rejection — a candle with a long wick pointing into the zone and a small body closing back outside — and the system fires an alert.

These zones represent levels where large buyers (demand) or large sellers (supply) previously stepped in with size. When price returns, those same institutions typically defend those levels again, creating a sharp, fast reaction that is visible on any timeframe.

Bullish zone rejection: price drops into a demand zone, prints a hammer (long lower wick), then rallies. Entry at the close of the rejection candle.

What to look for (Bullish)

  • Demand zone (Order Block or FVG) visible on chart
  • Price drops into the zone — wick or body enters
  • Rejection candle: lower wick ≥ 45% of total range
  • Candle body closes back above the zone top
  • Regime is bullish or neutral (not bearish + aligned)

What to look for (Bearish)

  • Supply zone (Order Block or FVG) visible on chart
  • Price spikes up into the zone
  • Rejection candle: upper wick ≥ 45% of total range
  • Candle body closes back below the zone bottom
  • Regime is bearish or neutral (not bullish + aligned)

What invalidates this setup

  • Price closes through the zone with a full body — that's a breakout, not a rejection
  • The stop-loss level is hit before any move in your direction
  • Regime is trending strongly against your trade direction (regime gate will suppress this alert)
  • Zone has already been tested multiple times and lost its "freshness"

Highest-probability version: A bullish zone rejection on MNQ where (1) the zone is an untested Order Block, (2) an open FVG overlaps the zone (+IOF bonus), (3) a BOS or CHoCH fired in the bullish direction in the prior 24 hours (+BOS qualifier), and (4) the 5m and 15m are both bullish (+alignment). This combination historically shows 77%+ win rate.

Alert Type 2

Prior Day High / Prior Day Low Breakout

Plain language: The Prior Day High (PDH) and Prior Day Low (PDL) are the highest and lowest prices reached during the previous trading day. These levels act as significant psychological and institutional reference points. When price closes beyond the PDH for the first time in the current session, it signals that the market has accepted a new, higher price range — a breakout continuation trade.

PDH/PDL breakouts are momentum trades. Institutional and algorithmic systems trigger above these levels (buy stops, momentum algos), adding fuel to the move. The key is that price must close beyond the level — a wick above does not confirm.

Bullish PDH breakout: price closes above the Prior Day High for the first time this session. The close signals institutional acceptance. Enter on the breakout candle's close.

Rules — PDH Breakout

  • Price closes above PDH (or below PDL for bearish) — a wick does not count
  • The prior candle's close was at or below PDH — this is the "first cross"
  • Only during RTH (Regular Trading Hours): 9:30 AM – 5:00 PM ET
  • Pre-market and after-hours PDH breaks are not flagged (institutional context doesn't apply)

Symbol-specific R:R targets

MNQ — 2:1 R:R

Nasdaq micro futures have larger ATR swings. PDH breakouts on MNQ tend to run significantly, making 2:1 R:R achievable and historically profitable (backtest: PF 5.36).

MGC — 1:1 R:R

Gold micro futures have tighter average ranges. MGC PDH breakouts reach 1:1 more reliably than 2:1. Taking profit sooner on MGC prevents giveback (backtest: PF 4.02).

MES — 2:1 R:R

Micro E-mini S&P 500 closely tracks MNQ but with lower volatility per point. PDH breakouts on MES behave similarly to MNQ — institutional acceptance of new range tends to run. Use for lower-risk exposure to S&P breakout setups.

MCL — 1:1 R:R

Micro Crude Oil is driven by supply/demand headlines and EIA reports. PDH breakouts on MCL are momentum-based but can reverse sharply on intraday news. 1:1 R:R reflects the tighter range and headline risk. Avoid MCL PDH setups within 30 minutes of an EIA release.

Delayed data advantage: PDH breakouts are the most delay-tolerant alert type. Institutional momentum from a clean PDH break typically runs for 30–90 minutes. Seeing a PDH breakout alert 15 minutes late often still presents a valid entry, especially if price has only extended modestly past the level and volume is expanding.

Alert Type 3

Trendline Break

Plain language: Price has been respecting a diagonal line — bouncing off it repeatedly (each bounce is a "touch"). When price finally closes on the other side of that line for the first time, it signals that the force maintaining that trend has broken down. What follows is often a sustained directional move as stop-losses trigger and new positions pile in.

The strength of a trendline break depends on how many times price respected the line before breaking it. A line with 5 touches is far more significant than one with 2. The system requires at least 3 touches before generating an alert.

Bullish trendline break: price has touched the resistance trendline 5 times (empty circles), each time retreating. The next candle finally closes above it — triggering the alert.

Why trendline breaks work with delayed data

Unlike a zone rejection (which happens in one candle and can resolve in minutes on fast timeframes), a trendline break launches a directional move that typically lasts many candles. By the time you see the alert 15 minutes later, you're often entering at the best possible point — after the initial volatile "break" candle, with confirmation that price is sustaining above (or below) the line.

Bullish trendline break

  • Resistance line has ≥ 3 prior touches
  • This candle closes ABOVE the projected line value
  • Prior candle closed AT or BELOW the line (fresh cross)
  • Best when regime is bullish or a CHoCH recently fired

Bearish trendline break

  • Support line has ≥ 3 prior touches
  • This candle closes BELOW the projected line value
  • Prior candle closed AT or ABOVE the line (fresh cross)
  • Best when regime is bearish or a CHoCH recently fired

What invalidates a trendline break

  • Price closes back through the broken trendline within 1–2 candles (false break)
  • The breakout candle is small and indecisive — the system scores break size, low scores flag marginal crosses
  • No volume expansion on the break (in live data mode)

When to enter with a delay: Pull up the current chart. If price has cleanly broken the trendline and is trading above/below it (not immediately back-testing it), enter at market. If price has shot far away from the trendline and the move looks extended, wait for a pullback to the trendline itself — that retest is often the cleanest entry anyway.

Alert Type 4

Opening Range Breakout

Plain language: The Opening Range (OR) is the high and low established in the first 30 minutes of the Regular Trading Hours session (9:30–10:00 AM ET). This range captures the initial tug-of-war between buyers and sellers at the open. When price closes decisively above the OR high or below the OR low after 10:00 AM ET, it signals that one side has taken clear control — and momentum tends to continue for at least the width of the OR range beyond the breakout level.

OR breakouts are among the most widely-watched intraday setups in equity futures. Institutional order flow concentrates in the first hour of RTH, making OR levels self-fulfilling reference points. Algorithms queue orders above/below these boundaries; a confirmed close through one releases that accumulated momentum.

Rules

  • OR must be fully formed — only fires after 10:00 AM ET (14:00 UTC)
  • Only within RTH — alerts suppressed after 3:30 PM ET (19:30 UTC) to avoid low-liquidity late-session noise
  • Price must CLOSE above OR high (bullish) or below OR low (bearish) — a wick alone does not qualify
  • First confirmed close only per session — repeated breaks of the same level after the first are ignored
  • Available on MNQ and MES (equity index instruments) — MCL and MGC do not use RTH opening range logic

Bullish OR Breakout

  • Entry: at the OR high level (the broken boundary)
  • Stop-loss: OR low (full OR range as stop distance)
  • Take profit: Entry + (OR range × 2) — 2:1 R:R
  • Best when regime is bullish and 5m/15m are aligned upward

Bearish OR Breakout

  • Entry: at the OR low level (the broken boundary)
  • Stop-loss: OR high (full OR range as stop distance)
  • Take profit: Entry − (OR range × 2) — 2:1 R:R
  • Caution: bearish OR breaks after 2 PM ET often fail — the day's trend frequently reasserts

Wide OR = wider stop: On high-volatility days (FOMC, NFP, CPI), the Opening Range can be 3–5× its average width. The alert's dollar risk display accounts for this — if it exceeds your max-risk budget, reduce contract size or skip the setup. The R:R target remains 2:1 regardless of OR width.

Calendar awareness: If a high-impact economic event fires within 15 minutes of the OR breakout trigger, the alert's confidence is reduced by 20 points and flagged with a ⚠ Near Event badge. Do not enter OR breakouts into an active event — wait for the move to settle first.

Highest-probability version: A bullish OR breakout on MNQ where (1) the regime is bullish on both 5m and 15m, (2) the break magnitude is strong (close well above OR high, not a tick-above), (3) an IOF zone sits just below the OR high (providing support after the break), and (4) no high-impact event within 15 minutes. This combination earns the full confidence stack.

Qualifier Signal

BOS & CHoCH — Structure Break Qualifiers

Break of Structure (BOS) and Change of Character (CHoCH) are not traded as standalone alerts — they are internal signals that improve zone rejection quality. They add +15 confidence points to any zone rejection that fires in the same direction within the following 24 hours.

BOS — Break of Structure

Trend continuation. Price closes beyond the last swing high (bullish BOS) or swing low (bearish BOS) in the direction of the current trend. Confirms momentum.

CHoCH — Change of Character

Reversal warning. Price closes beyond a swing point against the current trend. Signals the trend may be reversing. Unlocks counter-trend zone rejections when the regime gate would otherwise suppress them.

How they appear in alerts

When you see · BOS qualified or · CHoCH qualified in an alert rationale, it means a structural break fired in the same direction recently, and that context was used to boost the zone rejection's confidence by +15 points. These are among the highest-quality setups the system produces.

CHoCH and the regime gate: If the 5m regime is strongly bullish and aligned, the system normally suppresses bearish zone rejections. But if a bearish CHoCH fired recently, that suppression is lifted — the CHoCH is evidence that the structure has actually changed. A zone rejection tagged "CHoCH qualified" in a counter-trend direction is still a valid, high-quality setup.

Alerts below 65% are hidden by default because the noise-to-signal ratio below that threshold is too high.

Risk Management Rules

Even high-confidence setups fail. Risk management is what keeps you in the game long enough for your edge to compound.

Always use a stop-loss

Every alert includes an SL level. Place your stop there on entry. Never move a stop against you — moving stops is how losing trades become account-threatening trades.

Dollar risk display

The dollar risk shown on each card is (stop distance ÷ tick size) × tick value × contracts. If it shows in red, the setup exceeds your max-risk budget — reduce contracts or skip.

R:R Ratio

Default 2:1 (risk $1 to make $2). Adjustable per trade in the filter panel. Note: MGC PDH breakouts use a fixed 1:1 internally regardless of your setting — gold's range is tighter and 2:1 on MGC PDH produces worse outcomes.

One trade at a time

The system enforces one open trade per instrument. Don't stack multiple positions in the same direction — a directional move that reverses will hit all of them simultaneously.

Confidence thresholds as a position-sizing guide

  • 90–100% — Elite setup. Full allowed size. All confluences present.
  • 75–89% — High quality. Standard size. Regime + alignment + some confluence.
  • 65–74% — Moderate. Half size or wait for additional confirmation. Check context manually.
  • Below 65% — Hidden by default. Use for study only — not trading size.

Accounting for delayed data in risk management

  • Compare current price to the card's Entry level before entering
  • If price has already moved 50%+ of the way to TP, the setup has partly played out — skip or take a reduced position
  • If price has reversed past the SL level already, the trade is over — don't enter a dead setup
  • For trendline breaks: if price is back inside the broken trendline, the break failed — do not enter
  • 15-min delay affects 5m setups most — always verify current chart before entering a 5m alert

Never risk more than 1–2% of your account on a single trade. Use the contract count and max-risk settings to enforce this. Position sizing discipline is more important than setup quality — a single over-sized trade can erase weeks of gains.

Trend Indicators

EMA 9 ema9

The 9-period Exponential Moving Average — the fastest of the three. Tracks short-term momentum tick by tick. Price above EMA 9 is micro-bullish; price below is micro-bearish. Use it to gauge immediate trend direction within a candle sequence and to spot when short-term momentum is accelerating or fading.

EMA 21 ema21

The 21-period EMA. Medium-term trend direction. The 9/21 EMA pair is the primary trend lens used by most professional short-term traders — when EMA 9 crosses above EMA 21, it confirms a momentum shift. Zone rejections that align with the EMA 9/21 orientation are higher quality than those trading against it.

EMA 50 ema50

The 50-period EMA. Longer-term directional bias. Acts as dynamic support or resistance on the 15m and 30m timeframes. Price well above EMA 50 = confirmed uptrend context for the session. Useful as a macro bias filter before trading individual setups — e.g., only taking long zone rejections when price is above EMA 50.

VWAP vwap

Volume-Weighted Average Price — the most-watched intraday level by institutional traders. Calculated from the session open (price × volume). Algorithms and institutional desks treat VWAP as a fair-price benchmark: buying below VWAP is buying at value; selling above is selling at premium. A zone rejection that coincides with a VWAP reclaim or rejection carries extra conviction.

Prior Day High / Low priorDayHighLow

Horizontal lines at yesterday's RTH high and low. These are the reference levels for PDH/PDL Breakout alerts. When price closes above the PDH it signals range expansion; breaking PDL signals distribution or breakdown. Always worth keeping visible — they're critical context for any intraday directional trade.

Swing High / Low swingHighLow

Marked pivot points (local peaks and troughs) detected with a 10-candle lookback. These are the building blocks of market structure: BOS fires when a prior swing high is broken bullishly; CHoCH fires when a swing is taken out against the trend. Useful for manually reading structure and anticipating where breaks of structure may occur.

Auto Trendlines autoTrendlines

System-detected diagonal support and resistance lines anchored at swing extremes, requiring ≥3 touches to qualify. The touch count is tracked — more touches means a more significant line, and therefore a more significant break. Lines are refreshed each scan cycle. Trendline Break alerts fire when price closes through one of these.

Manual Trendlines manualTrendlines

Trendlines you draw directly on the chart by clicking two points. Stored in browser memory and persist across page reloads. Use them to mark your own key levels — diagonal channels, wedges, or higher-timeframe structures you're monitoring. Manual lines are visible only, they do not trigger alerts.

Order Flow (IOF) Layers

Order Blocks — Untested orderBlocksUntested

The last opposing candle before a strong impulsive move — e.g. the last bearish candle before a large rally. These represent levels where institutional orders are believed to be resting. An untested OB has never been revisited since formation — first touch is typically the highest-probability reaction. Shown as semi-transparent green (demand OB) or red (supply OB) rectangles.

Order Blocks — Tested orderBlocksTested

An OB that price has returned to and traded through at least once. Tested OBs carry less weight — some of the resting institutional orders have already been filled. Still useful as secondary support/resistance levels, but confidence in a strong rejection is lower than at an untested OB. Shown in a dimmer shade.

FVG — Open fvgOpen

A Fair Value Gap is a 3-candle imbalance where price moved so fast that a visible gap remains between the first candle's extreme and the third candle's extreme. An open FVG has not yet been revisited by price. They act as magnets (price tends to fill them) and as reaction zones. An open FVG overlapping a zone rejection earns the +IOF confidence bonus.

FVG — Filled fvgFilled

A previously open FVG where price has returned and traded into the gap, resolving the imbalance. Filled FVGs are less significant as magnets but can still act as minor support/resistance. Toggle off to reduce visual clutter if you prefer to only see unfilled gaps. Shown in a dimmer style to separate from open gaps.

Supply / Demand Zones supplyDemandZones

Composite shaded areas representing high-probability institutional zones — combines OB and FVG identification into larger visible areas where price has historically reversed. Zones are graded by freshness and proximity. Zone Rejection alerts fire when price enters and rejects from one of these areas.

IOF Confluence Zones iofConfluenceZones

Zones where two or more institutional order flow signals overlap — e.g. an OB inside an FVG, or an OB coinciding with a VWAP level. These are the highest-quality areas on the chart. The +IOF confidence bonus (+10 to +20) is calculated from IOF signals that overlap the zone at rejection time. "IOF" stands for Institutional Order Flow.

Market Depth Layers

CVD — Cumulative Volume Delta cvd

A synchronized sub-panel below the price chart showing buying vs. selling pressure estimated from OHLCV data. Each bar's delta is calculated as: volume × (2×(close−low)/(high−low) − 1) — positive (green) when close is near the high, negative (red) when near the low.
The histogram shows per-bar delta; the blue line is the cumulative sum, resetting at the RTH session open (9:30 AM ET) each day. Use CVD to confirm or question a price move: price rising with rising CVD = genuine buying; price rising with falling CVD = short-covering, not conviction. The sub-panel time scale is locked to the main chart.

QQQ Options Levels

When MNQ or MES is active, FuturesEdge AI overlays a set of options-derived reference levels on the chart. The data comes from QQQ (for MNQ) and SPY (for MES) — liquid ETFs whose options market provides far better data than NQ futures contracts directly. All QQQ strikes are automatically scaled to NQ futures price space using the live QQQ/NQ price ratio. Data is sourced from CBOE delayed quotes (free, 15-min delayed) and refreshed hourly.

Why QQQ instead of NQ futures? QQQ options are among the most actively traded options in the world. Tens of millions of contracts change hands daily. NQ futures options are thinly traded by comparison — the data is sparse and often meaningless. QQQ tracks the Nasdaq-100 index within fractions of a percent, so its options market is the real institutional positioning you want to see.

OI Walls — Open Interest Concentration

Open interest (OI) is the total number of options contracts that are still open and haven't been settled. Large OI at a strike means a lot of people — institutions, funds, hedgers — have positioned around that level. Market makers who sold those contracts are now delta-hedging, which creates mechanical buying and selling activity around those strikes.

Price approaching an OI Wall from below: Market makers are short calls at that strike. As price rises and those calls go ITM, MMs must buy futures to stay delta-neutral. This buying supports price and can act as a magnet pulling price up — until it breaks, at which point the forced buying accelerates the move.

Price approaching an OI Wall from above: Market makers are short puts at that strike. As price falls, MMs must sell futures to stay delta-neutral. This selling acts as a ceiling or resistance. A clean break below a major OI wall is often followed by acceleration downward.

OI Walls are gravitational, not absolute. Price frequently pins near the top OI wall into weekly expiration. But on high-momentum days (earnings, FOMC, CPI), the wall gives way. Use OI walls as context for where price is likely to stall or react — not as guaranteed turn points.

Max Pain

For every possible expiry strike, you can calculate how much total money option buyers would receive if price settled exactly there. Max pain is the strike where that total payout is lowest — meaning option sellers (primarily market makers and institutions who write options) collectively profit the most.

The theory: because large institutions with significant short-options exposure have the size to influence price action near expiry, price tends to gravitate toward the max pain strike in the days leading into expiration — particularly into the final 1–2 hours of trading on expiry day (Mon/Wed/Fri for QQQ).

How to use it: Max pain is most meaningful on expiration days. If price is well above max pain on a Wednesday, expect a gravitational pull downward during the afternoon session. If price is below max pain, expect a potential lift into close. On non-expiry days, treat it as a medium-term magnet — not an immediate target.

Gamma Flip (γ Flip)

Gamma measures how quickly a dealer's delta changes as price moves. When dealers are net long gamma (positive GEX), they automatically buy dips and sell rips to stay hedged — this is stabilizing. When dealers are net short gamma (negative GEX), they have to chase price in the same direction it's moving to re-hedge — this is destabilizing and amplifies volatility.

The gamma flip level is the price where dealer net gamma exposure crosses from positive to negative (or vice versa). It is the volatility regime boundary.

Price above γ Flip: Dealers are net long gamma. They act as shock absorbers — selling into strength, buying into weakness. Expect smaller intraday ranges, mean-reverting price action, and tighter ranges around key levels. Good environment for range setups and zone rejections.

Price below γ Flip: Dealers are net short gamma. They must chase price directionally to re-hedge — amplifying moves. Expect larger intraday ranges, trending price action, and levels breaking cleanly. Good environment for breakout and PDH/PDL setups.

The header bar gamma widget shows your current regime: ▲ Above γ Flip — low vol (teal) or ▼ Below γ Flip — high vol (red). On Mon/Wed/Fri, a 0DTE tag appears — gamma pinning effects are strongest on these days because short-dated options have the highest gamma per contract.

Call Wall and Put Wall

The call wall is the single strike above current price with the highest call OI. The put wall is the single strike below current price with the highest put OI. Together they define the current options-implied range — the zone where dealer hedging activity is most concentrated right now.

Practical use: The call wall is resistance, the put wall is support. Price tends to chop between them on low-momentum days. A clean close outside either wall — especially on volume — signals that options positioning has flipped and the move may extend further than expected.

QQQ Daily Reference Levels (PDO / PDC / DO)

These three levels anchor the current session relative to yesterday's price action. They're simple but among the most-watched levels by institutional desks:

QQQ PDO — Previous Day Open

Where yesterday's regular session began. A gap above PDO that holds signals bullish continuation from yesterday's open. A gap below PDO that fails to reclaim it signals bearish distribution. Less commonly referenced than PDC but useful for multi-day trend analysis.

QQQ PDC — Previous Day Close

The single most-watched reference level on any given day. Overnight futures pricing, institutional rebalancing, and pre-market activity all reference the prior close. Opening above PDC = bullish gap. Opening below PDC = bearish gap. Price often revisits PDC during the session as institutions flatten positions established overnight.

QQQ DO — Current Day Open

Where today's regular session opened. Holding above the day open = bulls in control of today's session. Falling below and failing to reclaim = bearish session structure. The day open is a live reference — it doesn't move. A powerful short-term anchor for bias: are we net up or net down since today began?

All levels are scaled from QQQ to NQ price space using the live QQQ/NQ price ratio at the time of the fetch (typically ~37–40×). The scaling is re-applied on every request so it stays accurate as the ratio shifts intraday. The widget refreshes every hour — if you want a manual refresh, switch symbols and back to MNQ.

  • P/C ratio: Put OI ÷ Call OI. Above 1.3 = heavy put buying = bearish skew or hedging. Below 0.7 = heavy call buying = bullish / speculative. 0.8–1.2 = neutral.
  • IV%: Implied volatility of the QQQ call strike nearest to current price. High IV means the market expects large moves. Low IV means calm expected. Compare to recent history — a sudden IV spike mid-session often precedes a sharp directional move.
  • MaxPain: The NQ-scaled max pain strike for the nearest QQQ expiry. Shown in NQ points so you can compare directly to the price on your chart.
  • γ Flip: The NQ level separating low-vol (above) from high-vol (below) dealer gamma regimes. The label updates live as price crosses the flip level.
  • 0DTE tag: Appears on Monday, Wednesday, and Friday — QQQ expiration days. Gamma effects are most pronounced; expect tighter pinning around max pain and OI walls into the close.

Features & Analysis Tools

The Features section (below layer toggles in the left panel) controls which server-side analysis modules are active. Changes are hot-applied — no server restart needed. The updated setting takes effect on the next scan cycle (every 5 minutes).

Volume Profile

Enables POC, VAH, and VAL calculation for current and prior RTH sessions. When off, volume profile lines disappear from the chart and the layer toggle has no effect. On by default.

Opening Range

Enables OR high/low/mid calculation and the OR Breakout alert type. When off, no OR lines are drawn and no or_breakout alerts are generated regardless of price action. On by default.

Performance Stats

Enables real-time win rate, profit factor, and average R calculations computed from all resolved alerts. Powers the Stats page. When off, /api/performance returns empty stats. On by default.

Economic Calendar

Fetches the weekly high-impact economic event schedule from ForexFactory's public feed (refreshed hourly). When enabled: a topbar countdown badge shows upcoming events within 3 hours for the active symbol; setup confidence is reduced −20 points if a high-impact event fires within 15 minutes of the trigger; affected cards show a ⚠ Near Event badge. On by default.

Relative Strength

Computes the session-normalized performance ratio between MNQ and MES using aligned 5-minute candles. Visible in the topbar widget when viewing MNQ or MES. Shows: ratio (1.0 = equal performance), correlation (rolling 20-period Pearson), and a directional signal. Ratio > 1.02 = MNQ leading (risk-on, bullish MNQ bias). Ratio < 0.98 = rotation to defensives (caution on MNQ longs). On by default.

Session Levels

Enables calculation of Asian session (00:00–07:00 UTC) and London session (07:00–13:30 UTC) high/low levels. When off, session level lines disappear from the chart and the layer toggle has no effect. On by default.

Alert Replay

Enables date-range filtering on the alerts API, which powers the Backtest/Replay page. When off, the replay page can only access alerts from the current server session rather than filtering by historical date range. On by default.

Correlation Heatmap

Enables the 4×4 pairwise correlation matrix calculation between all instruments. When off, the heatmap panel in the right sidebar is hidden and the /api/correlation endpoint returns no data. On by default.

Sound Alerts

Plays a two-tone audio chime when a new setup alert fires above your minimum confidence threshold. Uses the Web Audio API — no audio files required. Bullish alerts play an ascending tone (440→550 Hz); bearish play a descending tone (550→440 Hz). Off by default — requires explicit opt-in. Your browser may require a user interaction before allowing audio to play.

Push Notifications

Enables browser push notifications for new high-confidence alerts, delivered even when the tab is in the background. Requires granting notification permission in your browser when prompted. Off by default — requires explicit opt-in and browser permission grant.

Performance Page

Navigate to Stats in the topbar to view detailed performance analytics for all resolved alerts:

  • Summary cards: Overall win rate, profit factor, average R, total resolved trades
  • Bar charts: Win rate broken down by symbol, setup type, timeframe, and direction (bull vs. bear)
  • Time-of-day heat map: Win rate by UTC hour across RTH — green ≥60%, amber 40–60%, red <40%, gray = no data
  • Alert table: All resolved alerts with sortable columns — symbol, TF, type, direction, confidence, outcome, R achieved

Backtest & Replay Page

Navigate to Replay in the topbar to step through historical alerts within a custom date range:

  • Filters: Date range, symbol, setup type, minimum confidence threshold, contract size
  • Step-through navigation: Previous / Next buttons to walk through alerts one at a time; "View on Chart" loads that symbol and timeframe on the main dashboard
  • Equity curve: CSS bar chart showing cumulative P&L across the filtered alert set, using instrument point values (MNQ $2/pt, MES $5/pt, MGC $10/pt, MCL $100/pt)
  • Summary panel: Win rate, total R, and trade count for the filtered window

How to use Replay for learning: Set a date range from a week or month ago, filter to setups above 75% confidence, and step through each alert. For each one, ask yourself: "Would I have taken this? Why?" Then check the outcome. This builds intuition for what the system's high-confidence setups look like in real-time conditions.
