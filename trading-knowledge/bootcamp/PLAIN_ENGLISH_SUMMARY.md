# The Blind Monkey Bootcamp: A Plain-English Guide

*What 19 lessons of advanced futures trading education actually teach — explained for people who have never traded in their life*

---

## The Premise

Most people who try day trading lose money. Studies show somewhere around 90-95% fail within a year. The question this bootcamp tries to answer is: **why?** And more importantly: **what could a normal person do differently?**

The instructor — a guy named Matt who goes by "Rocket Scooter" — spent years figuring out that most trading education is nonsense. The classic advice ("find the trend," "cut your losses," "use indicators") sounds good but doesn't actually work reliably. His bootcamp takes a different approach: treat the stock market like a casino where the house edge is knowable, and teach students how to play odds the way a professional poker player would.

This document walks you through what the 19 lessons cover, explained in plain English.

---

## The Core Insight That Changes Everything

Traditional trading tries to **predict the price**. Someone looks at a chart, sees a pattern, and guesses "the price will go up from here." The bootcamp argues this is fundamentally impossible. Price is genuinely random on short timescales — no squiggle on a chart reliably tells you what the next squiggle will be.

But the bootcamp makes a subtle, powerful distinction: **price is random, but volume is predictable.**

Here's what that means. Every day, huge financial institutions (pension funds, hedge funds, investment banks) hold massive positions in the stock market. To protect those positions, they buy *options* — kind of like insurance policies that pay off if the market moves in a specific direction. These options force institutional traders to react in predictable ways at specific price levels. When the market hits certain levels, you can mathematically calculate that big money *has to* step in, whether they want to or not.

Think of it like this: imagine you could see a map of where every major landowner around a lake keeps their boats. You wouldn't know which boat would launch first, or when. But you'd know *where the boats are*. And if a storm came, you'd know *where people would run to rescue their boats*. That's what this framework does for the stock market. It doesn't predict the storm; it predicts where everyone will run.

The technical term is "options hedging." The practical tool is called a **Liquidity Map** — a visual display of where institutional money will react based on the options they've bought.

## What The Framework Actually Does

The first two-thirds of the bootcamp (Lessons 1-16) build a system of tools, all grounded in that core insight. Here's what each major piece does, in plain English:

**Liquidity Maps (Lesson 3)** show you where major financial institutions have their protective positions. These create "zones" — price levels where institutions are forced to buy or sell to rebalance their books. You want to buy near the bottom of a "Bull Zone" and sell near the top of a "Bear Zone."

**Monthly Hedge Pressure or MHP (Lessons 3, 7, 10)** is a specific price level — calculated from options math — where the market has the strongest tendency to bounce. Historically, when the market falls to MHP, it bounces about 90% of the time. This is the single strongest "I should buy here" signal in the system.

**Resilience (Lesson 4)** is a leading indicator. Before the stock market even opens each morning, Resilience tells you whether the day is likely to be green or red. It's based on how the market opens relative to where it closed.

**The DD Ratio and DD Band (Lesson 8)** measure whether the broader bull market is healthy or falling apart. The DD Ratio is literally a score from 0 to 1 showing what fraction of stocks are in bullish territory. Above 0.5 means the market is still a bull market. Below 0.5 is a warning sign. The DD Band is a pair of invisible walls that the market respects 88% of the time.

**The Commitment of Traders or COT Report (Lesson 6)** shows what Wall Street insiders are doing. Normally hedge funds are betting the market goes down (they're net short). Asset managers are betting it goes up (they're net long). When you watch how these groups shift their positions, you see the market's behind-the-scenes dynamics.

**Monthly Maps (Lesson 5)** extend the Liquidity Map concept to 3 months forward, showing where institutions are positioned for the whole upcoming quarter.

**VIX, BBB, and VVIX (Lesson 16 — the masterclass)** are volatility indicators. VIX measures fear. BBB (Bull Bear Breakpoint) is a calculated monthly threshold that tells you whether volatility is "cheap" or "expensive." VVIX is the volatility *of* the VIX — how sensitive fear itself is.

Here's the crucial insight from Lesson 16: **the first set of tools tells you WHICH direction to trade. The VIX tools tell you HOW to trade.** Are catalysts getting absorbed calmly, or amplified wildly? If VIX is above BBB and VVIX is above 100, the market is a "garbage month" — every small news event causes huge moves. Even if everything else says "buy," you should skip the month entirely if you're struggling. When both VIX indicators are calm AND the directional tools are bullish, you've got a "Golden Setup" — the rare month when trading is almost easy.

## The Three Things That Kill Traders

Around Lesson 17 (the framework's capstone), Matt boils every trader's failure down to three patterns. Every losing trader is doing at least one of them, usually two or three:

**First: Shorting too much.** About 70% of trading days are green (the market goes up). If you bet against the market half the time, you're fighting a mathematical headwind. The pain isn't even usually one huge loss — it's a slow drip of small losses, what Matt calls "a thousand paper cuts." Matt himself says he's long 80-90% of the time, even in bear markets.

**Second: Betting too big.** Most traders don't calculate how much they'll lose *before* they place the trade. When the loss comes, it's a shock to their psychology, not just their wallet. Matt has a simple test: if you come back to work Monday still hurting emotionally from Friday's loss, you were betting too big. The math is brutal — lose 50% of your account, and you need a 100% gain to recover. Most traders never make it back.

**Third: Overtrading.** Trading when there's no good reason to trade. This is the gambling impulse — money burning a hole in your pocket. The bootcamp teaches that the market is genuinely "irrational" sometimes (news chaos, panic selling, margin-call cascades) and during those periods, every tool in the framework fails. The skill is recognizing those periods and *sitting out*. Matt puts it bluntly: "A lot of you are gambling addicts and degenerates. Once money's in your hands, you can't wait to part with it."

## The Three Tenets

The bootcamp exists to solve exactly those three problems. Every tool maps to one of three "tenets":

1. **Trend Identification** — knowing which way the market is likely to go today (solves "shorting too much")
2. **Position Sizing and Risk Management** — knowing how much to bet (solves "betting too big")
3. **Market Avoidance** — knowing when to not trade at all (solves "overtrading")

That's it. Every Liquidity Map, every volatility indicator, every piece of math in the whole framework is in service of one of those three tenets.

## The Hidden Superpower: Knowing When NOT To Trade

This is the part that's missing from almost every other trading education, and it's what the bootcamp spends the most time on. Traditional technical analysis has a fatal flaw: the indicators always fire signals. An oversold signal in the middle of a market crash tells you to buy. But the market keeps crashing. The indicator has no way to say "I know I'm telling you to buy, but don't listen to me today."

The bootcamp's tools include explicit "sit out" rules:

- If volatility spikes too high in a single moment (the **VIX Risk Interval** rule), sit out
- If the market crosses certain thresholds (**Index Catalyst**), sit out
- If the market is within 5% of a circuit breaker, sit out
- If it's a "garbage month" with high volatility AND unstable volatility, consider skipping the entire month

Matt distinguishes between "structure" and "anti-structure." Structure is when traders are acting rationally — institutions executing planned exits, markets respecting support levels, patterns working. Anti-structure is panic — everyone running for exits with no plan, indicators firing randomly. In structured markets, the framework works. In anti-structured markets, nothing works. Recognizing the difference is a skill.

The instructor's 11-day winning streak during a bad market period came not from trading better, but from trading less — he was in the market only 5-10 minutes per trade, and spent most days doing nothing.

## The Practice Environment: Prop Firms

Here's where the last two lessons pivot. Lessons 18 and 19 are about something called a "Trader Funding Program" (TFP), also known as a "prop firm."

The concept is strange but clever: specific companies will let you trade a simulated account. If you hit certain profit targets without blowing up, they pay you real money — treating your trade data as "market research" they're buying from you. It's a legal loophole that's been booming since 2020. You pay a small monthly fee (like $100-250), and in return you get the chance to earn real payouts on paper trades.

Why does this matter? Because the bootcamp argues that learning to trade requires one specific thing: **real emotional stakes.** Pure paper trading (with no money at risk) teaches nothing because there's no fear. Pure real-money trading works, but costs most people tens of thousands of dollars in tuition. Prop firms are the middle ground — enough stakes to create the learning environment, but only $100-250 a month instead of $10,000.

Matt frames this as **tuition**. Set a monthly budget with your spouse or partner *before* you sign up — say, $2,000 a month. Commit to a two-year education. Assume all of it will be gone by year-end. The goal isn't to get rich from prop firms; it's to learn.

Lesson 19 (the final lesson) teaches how to actually play each type of prop firm account. There are three flavors, each requiring a completely different style:

- **Static accounts** give you a fixed loss limit. First trade is your best trade. Wait for perfect conditions. Buy only on 80-90% off sales.
- **Trailing accounts** punish you for winning the "wrong way." If you're up $10,000 and then lose $3,000, you can blow up even though you're still profitable. Play base hits only, never home runs.
- **End-of-day accounts** (Matt's preferred type) let you trade normally but require patience — you have to trade for 5-14 days to earn a payout.

Matt teaches "rule bypasses" — ways to beat firms at their own game. The most memorable: one of the hardest rules is "consistency" (limiting how much you can make in a single day). Matt's counter-strategy is to *intentionally break the consistency rule* on your first cycle. This forces you to trade longer but creates a massive financial buffer. After that, the next several payout cycles become effectively risk-free. This technique is how he built a $200,000 buffer across 5 accounts.

Most importantly, he repeatedly emphasizes a simple rule: **"Any rule a firm bans is because it works."** If they say "no adding to losing trades," it's because adding to losing trades is profitable. If they say "no high-frequency trading," it's because someone figured out a bot-based exploit. The rules aren't protecting you; they're protecting the firm from proven strategies.

## The Ultimate Goal (The Real Punchline)

Here's what makes Lesson 19 extraordinary. Matt is an affiliate of several prop firms — he gets paid when students sign up for the firms he recommends. His financial incentive is to tell you to stay in the prop firm ecosystem forever, because he earns commissions as long as you do.

Instead, in the closing minutes of the final lesson, he says:

> "Nobody, and I mean nobody, makes a living out of prop trading. Zero people. Your ultimate goal to trade props is to not need them."

The argument is mathematical. Prop firm payouts are treated as wage income, taxed at up to 37% (your highest tax bracket). Plus firms take 10-50% of your profits as their cut. Net retention: 40-60 cents on every dollar you earn.

Real futures trading (in your own brokerage account) is taxed at a 60/40 blended rate, effectively around 20%. No profit split. Net retention: about 80 cents on every dollar.

So the whole bootcamp is really about this arc:

1. **Learn the framework** (Lessons 1-17)
2. **Budget for prop firms as tuition** (Lesson 18)
3. **Master the prop firm game** (Lesson 19)
4. **Use prop firm payouts to fund your own trading account**
5. **Exit the prop firm ecosystem entirely**

Prop firms are training wheels, not the destination. Matt admits openly: his own real brokerage account generates vastly more income than his prop firm payouts — "laughably far" more, in his words.

## The Major Takeaways

If you were to extract ten insights from the entire 19-lesson bootcamp that apply to life, money, or decision-making broadly, these would be them:

**1. Price is random. Volume is predictable.** You can't predict what the stock market will do in the next hour, but you can predict where big money will be *forced* to react. Similar patterns appear in other domains — you can't predict what an individual will do, but you can predict what populations will do.

**2. You control losses, not wins.** In any probabilistic endeavor, you don't decide when the good outcomes happen. But you absolutely decide how much you're willing to lose in pursuit of them. Matt's ultimate framework distillation: "You have 100% control over how much you're willing to lose. The market can never take that away from you."

**3. Your edge is discipline, not prediction.** The framework doesn't give you a crystal ball. It gives you a systematic way to bet smaller when uncertain, bigger when confluences align, and not at all when signals are irrational. This is how a casino runs: it doesn't predict individual hands, it manages the odds across millions of hands.

**4. Most of the work is avoiding mistakes.** Three failure modes cover most trader losses: shorting too much, betting too big, overtrading. The framework is primarily designed to prevent these, not to find brilliant trades.

**5. Knowing when NOT to act is a superpower.** Most skills emphasize action. Trading skill is largely about inaction — recognizing when conditions don't favor you and sitting out. Traditional indicators can't do this; humans can. The skill is the difference between someone who trades 200 times a year and someone who trades 20 times a year, with the latter often outperforming.

**6. Structure requires rationality.** Tools only work when participants are acting rationally. When everyone panics (margin calls, news chaos), structure breaks down and normal methods fail. Recognizing "anti-structure" periods and stepping away is itself a skill.

**7. Real learning requires real stakes.** Pure simulation teaches nothing. Pure real-money trading costs too much. Find the smallest stakes that still create emotional engagement — that's where actual skill is built. This applies far beyond trading: it's why homework with no consequences rarely teaches as well as projects with real outcomes.

**8. Any rule a system bans exists because that thing works.** Prop firms ban specific strategies not because those strategies are bad but because they're *effective*. The rules are reactive to proven exploits. This is a general pattern — rules tell you what works by showing what's prohibited.

**9. Budget for education like tuition.** Set a monthly amount, commit to a timeframe, assume it's gone by year-end, get partner/spouse buy-in before starting. Treat skill acquisition as an investment with uncertain returns and a known cost, not a get-rich-quick scheme.

**10. The ultimate goal is to not need the system.** The bootcamp exists to teach you how to master prop firms so you can eventually leave them entirely. Training wheels come off. This is a counter-intuitive endpoint for an education product, but it's the honest one. The goal of any real education should be making itself obsolete.

## A Closing Thought

What's striking about this bootcamp, stepping back, is how much of it isn't really about trading. The technical tools (Liquidity Maps, MHP, VIX) fill about half the content. The other half is about:

- Emotional discipline
- Budget management and household agreements
- Knowing when to stop trying
- Recognizing failure patterns in yourself
- Long-term financial architecture
- Being honest about what money is and what it costs you

Matt's final quote frames the whole thing:

> "You have 100% control over how much you're willing to lose. And no matter what, the market can never take that away from you."

Replace "market" with almost any uncertain endeavor — a business, a relationship, a health goal, a creative project — and the principle still holds. You don't control the wins. You control the losses. Master the loss side, and the wins take care of themselves over enough repetitions.

That's what 19 lessons of advanced futures trading actually teach.
